What Makes an All-Star

Introduction

NBA players can be grouped into different categories; prospect, role player, and star. Stars seem to have a quality that distinguishes them from other players. Maybe that special attribute is their stats. Or maybe, it is leadership and team success. Could it be popularity? Or maybe it is something else entirely. We will use a data science and machine learning approach to attempt to find out what makes a star. Since the definition of a star can be subjective, the definition of a star will be the same as an all-star for the analysis.

Imports and Functions

Python packages are imported for deep learning and machine learning, particularily scikit-learn and tensorflow with the Keras API. Pandas and numpy are used for cleaning and various other tasks, while seaborn and matplotlib are used to generate visualizations.

Dataset

A dataset with 19374 rows and 67 columns is loaded into the workspace. This includes player data starting from the 1979-80 season, since this is the season that the 3 points line was introduced. Each row represents an individual player's season, and each column represents a statistic. Statistics include per-game averages such as points per game, totals such as the number of games played, and advanced stats such as usage percentage. Each observation of a player's season includes a label that indicated whether or not the player was an all-star during the given season. This is the data that will be used to train and evaluate the all-star prediction models.

Models

The models learn patterns from the data to make predictions on the desired output. These are machine learning models, so they make decide on how to best make predictions without my input. In this case, the desired output is whether or not the player is an all-star. They all take different strategies to learn from the data, but ultimately have the same goal. All of these models use supervised learning approches, meaning that the training data has input and output examples. The 3 model types include logistic regression, neural network, and random forest model types.

Logistic Regression Model

A logistic regression model predicts a variable with only 2 possible values. It follows a simple decision rule based on a probability to decide the classification (in this case, All-Star or non All-Star). This is the simplest type of model and All-Star predictions are likely to be less nuanced than the other models.

This approach favours high volume scorers who take a lot of shots and get to the line consistently. Interestingly, the model favours a high volume of 2-point attempts over 3-point attempts, and favours free-throw attempts over free-throws made. This is likely influenced by dominant dominant All-Star big men such as Shaquille O'Neal and Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, who draw a lot of contact and score a lot of 2's.

Neural Network Sequential Model

The neural network learns from the data and determines the importance of each stat in predicting all-stars on its own. It consists of a sequence of layers that continually pass data through. The features are assigned weights (importance) to ultimately output a probability that a player is an all-star.

The neural network places great importance on VORP (Value Over Replacement Player), when predicting an All-Star. It essentially compares a player's performance to the performance of an average player. Logically this makes sense, it supports the theory that an All-Star stands out among peers. Offensive win shares and defensive win shares also stand out as important statistics. These are measure a player's impact on winning, and favour players on winning teams. Games played are also important, which supports the theory that All-Stars are usually players who have been available for their teams. As the saying goes, availability is the best ability. An interesting feature that the model valued is turnovers per game (TOV). Looking at the numbers, it tends to be the best players that lead the league in turnovers. Usually, only players that teams trust are able to have enough possession and decision making capabilities to cause enough turnovers.

Random Forest Model

The random forest model consists of a group of 200 decison trees, and makes predictions based off the outcome of the decision trees. Each decision tree consists of many splits (ex. points per game above or below 20 ppg), and makes the final decision based on the splits.

The random forest model also values VORP and winning (in this case, win shares), although it values points more than the neural network. It is also the first model that values efficiency as an important attribute, in this case PER (Player Efficiency Rating). So it is valid to conclude that efficiency may have some value in predicting All-Stars although it is clearly not significant considering that it does not appear in any of the other models. An interesting point is that the model also highly values BPM (Box Plus Minus). BPM is essentially the same as VORP, aside from the fact that it does not account for playing time. This stat was notably absent from the neural network's most important features, and highlights the difference in the model's decision making process.

Ensemble Model

The ensemble model uses the 3 models (logistic regression, neural network, and random forest) to form the final all-star prediction as the most popular prediction. Ensemble predictions are less likely to give unreasonable results since they have to be shared by at least 2 of the models to make a conclusion.

We can further evaluate the models by comparing differences in specific statistics between true all-stars and falsely predicted all-stars. On average, true all-stars have higher offensive impact and opportunity. It is important to note that true all-stars generally include all of the established superstars, so numbers will be skewed by the top few players. This does not however, explain why defensive metrics seem to be in favour of falsely predicted all-stars. There are a few ways to interpret the higher defensive metrics for false all-stars:

  1. Defensive players are undervalued during all-star selection
  2. The models overvalue defensive impact
  3. These are anomlies

Looking at the table of falsely predicted all-stars, a few names stand out in terms of defensive impact. Potential explanations for not being selected to all-star teams could be that they were overshadowed by teammates or that their box score statistics were not as prominent as others.

All-Star Stats

Based on the feature importance determined by the models, VORP (Value Over Replacement Player) and OWS (Offensive Win Shares) appear to be some of the most important features. VORP and win share statistics were significant in both the neural network and random forest models. Among the win share statistics, offensive win shares are likely more important due to the conclusion that we determined earlier, that offensive metrics are more valued for all-stars than defensive ones. The graph below plots VORP against OWS for each player in the top 30 highest VORP players. The aim is to discover what relationship exists between the two statistics, and how it impacts all-star selection.

In the plot, green dots represent all-stars, while red dots represent non all-stars. It seems that players with relatively lower VORP and OWS are less likely to be selected to all-star teams, as expected. A key factor in Victor Wembanyama missing the cut also becomes apparent; his offensive win share statistic is an large negative outlier compared to other players with high VORP. This reinforces the hypothesis that offensive impact is more valued than defensive impact for all-stars, and supports the hypothesis that contributing to a winning team is important for the selection. Notable players with relatively low VORP and OWS that were all-stars include Donovan Mitchell, Anthony Edwards, Paul George, and Tyrese Maxey. Their selection may be more closely related to their standout box score statistics. The last clear anomoly is Domantis Sabonis, who clearly excels in both statistics, yet misses the all-star cutoff. This may be because of his weaknesses defensively which goes against the hypothesis that offense is more valued than defense.

Conclusions

There are several conclusions about the all-star selection process from this analysis. Some of the important conclusions about NBA all-stars that we discovered in this analysis are as follows:

  1. All-stars have high VORP, points, and contribute on winning teams
  2. Offensive output, particularily scoring, is usually favoured over defensive metrics in all-star selections
  3. All-stars bring multiple or unique skills to the court that usually require several role players to replace
